Must & Should

Obligation · prohibition · advice

must, have to, should

Three modals for obligation and advice — each with a different level of strength.

must
Strong internal obligation — I feel this is necessary: I must call her.
have to
External rule or requirement — it comes from outside: I have to wear a uniform.
should
Advice or recommendation — a good idea: You should see a doctor.

must vs have to

Internal obligation vs external rule

must — I decide
I must study tonight.
my own decision
You must try this restaurant!
strong recommendation
have to — rule
I have to wear a helmet.
law/rule from outside
She has to start at 9.
company rule
In everyday spoken English, have to and must are often interchangeable. The distinction matters most in formal writing.

Negatives: the critical difference

mustn't vs don't have to — opposite meanings!

mustn't = prohibited
You mustn't park here.
= it is NOT allowed
You mustn't tell anyone.
= forbidden
don't have to = not necessary
You don't have to come.
= optional, up to you
You don't have to hurry.
= there's no rush
Critical: mustn't = FORBIDDEN. Don't have to = NOT REQUIRED (but OK if you want). Confusing these changes the meaning completely.

should / shouldn't — advice

Weaker than must — a good idea, not an order

Advice
You should eat more vegetables.
Recommendation
You should see that film — it's brilliant.
Negative advice
You shouldn't stay up so late.
Asking for advice
Should I call her?
ought to
Ought to = same meaning as should (more formal).

had better — urgent advice

Stronger than should — implies a bad consequence

had better + base
You had better hurry — the train leaves in 5 minutes!
Contraction
You'd better call her now.
Negative
You had better not be late.
"Had better" always takes the base verb (not "had better to go"). And "had" here is not past — it refers to the present/future.

Common mistakes

You mustn't wear a uniform. (meaning: it's not required)
→
You don't have to wear a uniform.
mustn't = forbidden · don't have to = not required
You should to see a doctor.
→
You should see a doctor.
Modals never take "to"
She musts go now.
→
She must go now.
Modals never take -s
I have to study — it's my choice.
→
I must study — it's my choice.
Own choice → must, not have to
