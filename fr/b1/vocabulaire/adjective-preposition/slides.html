Common combinations that follow adjectives in English.

B1 Vocabulary

Adjectives followed by prepositions

In English, certain adjectives are always followed by specific prepositions. Learning these combinations helps you speak and write more naturally.

For example:

  • Interested in: Are you interested in sports?
  • Good at: She is good at mathematics.
  • Angry with: He is angry with his brother.
  • Afraid of: They are afraid of spiders.

These combinations cannot be changed. You cannot say "interested on" or "good for" — the preposition is fixed.

Common patterns: -ED adjectives + preposition

Adjectives ending in -ed (often past participles) typically use these prepositions:

Interested inI am interested in film history.
Worried aboutShe was worried about the exam.
Excited aboutThey are excited about the holiday.
Annoyed with / atHe was annoyed with the noise.
Focused onWe must stay focused on our goal.
Tired ofI am tired of waiting.

Common patterns: Ability & emotion + preposition

Good atHe is good at cooking.
Bad atI am bad at swimming.
Afraid ofShe is afraid of heights.
Angry withHe is angry with me.
Jealous ofShe felt jealous of her sister.
Proud ofThey are proud of their daughter.

Common patterns: Relationship + preposition

Similar toThis car is similar to mine.
Different fromToday is different from yesterday.
Opposite toHis view is opposite to mine.
Dependent onSuccess is dependent on hard work.
Based onThe film is based on a true story.
Aware ofAre you aware of the new rules?

Exercices

Complete these sentences with the correct preposition:

1. She is skilled _____ playing the piano. (at)

2. I am not responsible _____ that mistake. (for)

3. Are you familiar _____ this author's work? (with)

4. The garden is full _____ flowers. (of)

5. He is confident _____ his abilities. (in)

Key takeaway

Adjective + preposition combinations are fixed in English. Memorise them as chunks, not as separate words. When you learn a new adjective, always check which preposition follows it.
